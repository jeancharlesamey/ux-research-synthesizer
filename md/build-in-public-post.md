# Build in Public Post

## Purpose

Build-in-public works only when the posts are useful to other people, not status updates about yourself. This skill produces posts that teach something specific, with the project as the example — not posts about the project that happen to mention a learning.

## When to use

- Shipping a v1 or major update
- Hitting a meaningful technical or product milestone
- End of a month/phase in the Skill Path program
- After a user test that surprised you
- When you broke something and learned from it

## Inputs

1. What happened (the event the post is about)
2. What you learned that you didn't know before
3. What you'd tell someone considering doing the same thing
4. Whether there's a concrete artifact to link (repo, deployed app, doc)

## Post types

Pick exactly one type per post. Mixing types produces unfocused writing.

### Type A — The launch
You shipped something. Public URL or repo exists.

Structure:
- **What I shipped** (one sentence, one URL)
- **Why I built it** (the actual problem, not "I wanted to learn X")
- **The one decision I'm proud of** (a technical or design choice, with the reasoning)
- **The one decision I'm not sure about** (be honest)
- **What I'd love feedback on** (specific, not "what do you think")
- **Link** (the URL again at the end)

Length: 400–700 words.

### Type B — The learning
You hit a wall and learned something general from it.

Structure:
- **The situation** (1 paragraph — concrete, the actual thing that happened)
- **What I thought would work** (and why — show your reasoning)
- **What actually happened** (the disconnect)
- **What I changed** (the new mental model, not just the new code)
- **Where this applies beyond my project** (this is what makes the post useful to others)

Length: 600–900 words.

### Type C — The retrospective
End of a month or phase. Multiple things to cover.

Structure:
- **The month in one sentence** (the headline)
- **What I shipped** (2–4 bullets, with links)
- **What I learned** (the 1 or 2 things worth remembering — not a list of 10)
- **What I'd do differently** (one specific thing)
- **What's next** (the next month, one sentence)

Length: 500–800 words.

### Type D — The opinion
You have a take. The project is the evidence.

Structure:
- **The claim** (one sentence, declarative, falsifiable)
- **Why people get this wrong** (the common position)
- **What changed my mind** (your specific experience — this is where the project shows up)
- **The implication** (what someone should do differently)
- **The honest counter** (what would make you wrong)

Length: 600–1000 words.

## Writing rules

- One declarative sentence per paragraph average. Punchy.
- No em dashes (style choice for JC's voice)
- No hedge words ("just", "kind of", "I think maybe"). State the claim or don't.
- No marketing words ("revolutionary", "game-changer", "10x")
- Show specific numbers, durations, code snippets when relevant
- Quote yourself or others when the exact wording matters
- One screenshot or code block per ~300 words, max
- End with something concrete the reader can do or click

## Anti-patterns to refuse

- Posts that are 80% personal narrative and 20% useful content
- "Here's what I'm working on" with no link to anything
- Lists of 10 lessons learned (force a cut to 1 or 2)
- LinkedIn-style humble-brag structures ("I failed. I learned. I grew.")
- Posts that announce future posts instead of saying the thing now
- Apologizing for not posting in a while (just post)

## Voice calibration for JC

Based on past writing preferences:
- Punchy declarative sentences
- No em dashes
- Additive framing ("and" over "but")
- Honest self-critique without self-flagellation
- Concrete specificity over general principles
- Org-level perspective on design × AI

## The honest test

After drafting, ask: "Would I forward this to a friend who is not following my journey?" If no, the post is too inward-facing. Cut the personal narrative until the answer is yes.
